The Role of an Army Chaplain

An Army Chaplain is a commissioned officer and religious leader who provides spiritual support and guidance to soldiers and their families. Their primary mission is to ensure that all service members can freely exercise their religion, a right guaranteed by the U.S. Constitution. Chaplains serve as part of a Unit Ministry Team (UMT), which also includes a Religious Affairs Specialist, an enlisted soldier who provides security and support.

Main Responsibilities

The duties of an Army Chaplain are multifaceted, encompassing religious services, counseling, and advising command on spiritual and moral matters. Their responsibilities fall under three core competencies:

  • Nurture the Living — Chaplains develop and execute religious support plans to strengthen soldiers and their families, including worship services and religious education.
  • Care for the Wounded — Chaplains provide comfort and hope to those injured in body, mind, or spirit, offering pastoral care to the sick and dying.
  • Honor the Fallen — Chaplains conduct memorial services and funerals, honoring soldiers who have made the ultimate sacrifice.

Chaplains are non-combatants and do not carry weapons. Their role is to provide a ministry of presence in all circumstances.

Confidentiality

One of the most critical aspects of a chaplain's work is the principle of 100% confidentiality. This is a legal and sacred trust — any communication with a chaplain or their assistant is privileged and protected from disclosure, with no exceptions for reporting even in cases of criminal activity or threats of harm. The privilege belongs to the individual and only they can waive it.
